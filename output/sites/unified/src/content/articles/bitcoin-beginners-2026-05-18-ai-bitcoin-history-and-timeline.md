## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. What was Bitcoin’s price when it first became usable for purchases?

The first recorded commercial transaction occurred on **May 22 2010**. The buyer paid **10,000 BTC** for two pizzas, implying a price of roughly **$0.003 per BTC** (based on the prevailing BTC‑USD exchange rate of the time). This figure is widely cited from Laszlo Hanyecz’s forum post on bitcointalk.org (source: Bitcoin Wiki).

### 2. How many times has Bitcoin’s block reward halved, and what were the rewards?

Bitcoin has experienced **four** halving events (as of April 2024). The sequence is:

| Halving Date | Block Height | Reward (BTC) |
|--------------|--------------|--------------|
| Nov 28 2012  | 210,000      | 25.0         |
| Jul 09 2016  | 420,000      | 12.5         |
| May 11 2020  | 630,000      | 6.25         |
|Apr 19 2024   | 840,000      | 3.125        |

Each halving reduces the new supply of BTC by 50 %, historically correlating with later price appreciation (source: Blockchain.com halving data).

### 3. What is the significance of the 2021 market‑cap milestone?

On **April 5 2021**, Bitcoin’s total market capitalization crossed **$1 trillion** for the first time, driven by a price rise to **≈$55,000**. This event signaled Bitcoin’s transition from a niche asset to a mainstream store of value, prompting institutional adoption (e.g., MicroStrategy’s $4.5 billion purchase) and ETF filings (source: CoinDesk Market Cap Index).

### 4. Why does the narrative history include technical upgrades like SegWit and Taproot?

Technical upgrades affect Bitcoin’s **scalability, security, and privacy**, which in turn influence price and adoption. For example:

- **SegWit (August 2017)** increased block capacity by moving signature data outside the main block, enabling the **Lightning Network** and reducing transaction fees by ~30 % (source: Bitcoin.org).  
- **Taproot (November 2021)** introduced smart‑contract flexibility while preserving privacy, making complex transactions look identical to simple transfers, which can improve fungibility and reduce on‑chain load (source: Bitcoin Optech).

Understanding these upgrades helps investors gauge long‑term utility and potential future demand.
