# Best Index Funds for Beginners

The top index funds for beginners are **Vanguard Total Stock Market Index Fund (VTI)**, **Fidelity 500 Index Fund (FXAIX)**, and **Schwab S&P 500 Index Fund (SWPPX)**, because they offer the lowest expense ratios (all under **0.04%**), provide broad market exposure, and have a long‑term annualized return of roughly **12%** over the past decade (as of Dec 2024). These three funds are also available as both mutual funds and ETFs, making them accessible to investors with any account size.

---

## Vanguard Total Stock Market Index Fund (VTI)


![Hero image for best index funds for beginners](https://picsum.photos/seed/best-index-funds-for-beginners-hero/1200/630)


- **Ticker:** VTI (ETF) / VTSAX (mutual fund)  
- **Expense Ratio:** **0.03%** (VTI) / **0.04%** (VTSAX)  
- **Assets Under Management (AUM):** **$1.1 trillion** (as of Dec 2025)  
- **Minimum Investment:** $0 for ETF; $3,000 for mutual fund  
- **10‑Year Annualized Return:** **12.4%** (as of Dec 2024, Vanguard)  
- **Morningstar Rating:** **5‑Star** (Overall)  

### Pros
- **Ultra‑low cost** – one of the cheapest ways to own the entire U.S. equity market.  
- **Instant diversification** – holds ~3,600 stocks across all market caps.  
- **High liquidity** – ETF trades like a stock with tight bid‑ask spreads.  

### Cons
- **Slightly higher expense ratio** for the mutual‑fund share class (0.04% vs. 0.03%).  
- **No exposure to international markets** – you’ll need a separate fund for global diversification.

## Fidelity 500 Index Fund (FXAIX)

- **Ticker:** FXAIX  
- **Expense Ratio:** **0.015%** (one of the lowest among U.S. large‑cap funds)  
- **AUM:** **$460 billion** (Dec 2025)  
- **Minimum Investment:** $0 (no minimum for Fidelity investors)  
- **10‑Year Annualized Return:** **12.1%** (as of Dec 2024, Fidelity)  
- **Morningstar Rating:** **5‑Star** (Large‑Blend category)  

### Pros
- **Near‑zero expense ratio** – maximizes net returns.  
- **No minimum investment** – ideal for investors starting with small amounts.  
- **Strong performance history** – consistently tracks the S&P 500 with minimal tracking error.  

### Cons
- **Only large‑cap exposure** – misses mid‑ and small‑cap growth opportunities.  
- **No international component** – requires pairing with an international index fund for worldwide coverage.

## Schwab S&P 500 Index Fund (SWPPX)


![Illustration for best index funds for beginners](https://picsum.photos/seed/best-index-funds-for-beginners-mid/1200/630)


- **Ticker:** SWPPX  
- **Expense Ratio:** **0.02%**  
- **AUM:** **$370 billion** (Dec 2025)  
- **Minimum Investment:** $0 (no minimum for Schwab brokerage accounts)  
- **10‑Year Annualized Return:** **12.0%** (as of Dec 2024, Schwab)  
- **Morningstar Rating:** **5‑Star** (Large‑Blend category)  

### Pros
- **Very low cost** – only $0.20 per $1,000 invested annually.  
- **No account minimum** – accessible to any beginner.  
- **Seamless integration** with Schwab’s banking and advisory tools.  

### Cons
- **S&P 500 focus only** – limited to 500 large U.S. companies.  
- **Slightly higher expense ratio** than FXAIX (0.02% vs. 0.015%).

## iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV)

- **Ticker:** IVV  
- **Expense Ratio:** **0.03%**  
- **AUM:** **$320 billion** (Dec 2025)  
- **Price per Share:** **~$525** (as of Jan 2026)  
- **10‑Year Annualized Return:** **12.2%** (as of Dec 2024, iShares)  
- **Morningstar Rating:** **5‑Star**  

### Pros
- **Excellent liquidity** – tight spreads and high average daily volume.  
- **Broad U.S. large‑cap coverage** – mirrors the S&P 500 index precisely.  
- **Low cost** – comparable to Vanguard’s VTI but with a slightly lower AUM.  

### Cons
- **Higher per‑share price** may deter fractional‑share investors (though fractional shares are often available).  
- **No small‑ or mid‑cap exposure** – still need a separate fund for full market coverage.
