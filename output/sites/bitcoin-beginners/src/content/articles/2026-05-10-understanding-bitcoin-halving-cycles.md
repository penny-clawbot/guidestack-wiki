# Understanding Bitcoin Halving Cycles: How They Affect Price, Supply, and Mining

The cryptocurrency world operates on predictable cycles that have fascinated investors, miners, and financial analysts for over a decade. Among these cycles, Bitcoin halving stands out as one of the most significant events in the entire crypto ecosystem. Every four years, Bitcoin undergoes a programmed reduction in its block reward, fundamentally altering the economics of mining and the dynamics of supply entering the market. Understanding Bitcoin halving cycles is essential for anyone looking to navigate the complex world of cryptocurrency investment and blockchain technology.

Since Bitcoin's inception in 2009, there have been three complete halving events, with each one triggering substantial market movements and reshaping investor sentiment. The first halving occurred in November 2012, reducing the reward from 50 BTC to 25 BTC per block. The second took place in July 2016, dropping the reward to 12.5 BTC, and the third happened in May 2020, bringing rewards down to 6.25 BTC. Each of these events preceded dramatic price increases that captured global attention and brought millions of new participants into the cryptocurrency market. According to historical data from CoinMarketCap, Bitcoin's price increased by approximately 8,000% in the twelve months following the 2012 halving, while the 2016 halving preceded a price surge that eventually saw Bitcoin reach nearly $20,000 in late 2017.

The significance of halving cycles extends far beyond simple price speculation. These events represent a core architectural decision embedded in Bitcoin's source code by its anonymous creator, Satoshi Nakamoto. The mechanism serves multiple purposes, including controlling inflation, creating scarcity, and ensuring the long-term viability of the network. For beginners entering the cryptocurrency space, comprehending halving cycles provides a foundation for understanding Bitcoin's economic model and its potential as a store of value. This comprehensive guide will walk you through everything you need to know about Bitcoin halving cycles, their historical context, their impact on price and supply, and how they affect the mining ecosystem that keeps the Bitcoin network running.

## What is Bitcoin Halving?

Bitcoin halving refers to the programmed reduction of the reward that miners receive for validating transactions and adding new blocks to the blockchain. This event occurs approximately every 210,000 blocks, which translates to roughly every four years, depending on the total computing power securing the network. The halving mechanism is hardcoded into Bitcoin's protocol, meaning it happens automatically without requiring intervention from any central authority, developer, or organization. This predictable schedule is one of Bitcoin's most distinctive features, as it creates a transparent and verifiable monetary policy that cannot be altered retroactively.

The mathematical foundation behind halving cycles ensures that the total supply of Bitcoin will never exceed 21 million coins. With each halving, the rate at which new Bitcoin enters circulation decreases exponentially. When Bitcoin launched in 2009, miners received 50 BTC for every block they added to the blockchain. After the first halving, this dropped to 25 BTC. After the second, it became 12.5 BTC, and after the third, it settled at 6.25 BTC. The next halving, expected in 2024, will reduce this reward to 3.125 BTC per block. This diminishing reward schedule creates an increasingly scarce asset, as the inflation rate of Bitcoin drops with each halving event.

Understanding the technical mechanics of halving requires familiarizing yourself with how blocks are added to the Bitcoin blockchain. Miners use specialized computer hardware to solve complex mathematical puzzles, a process known as Proof of Work, which secures the network and validates transactions. When a miner successfully solves the cryptographic puzzle for a block, they receive the current block reward as compensation for their computational effort and electricity consumption. The halving event reduces this reward by exactly 50%, making mining less immediately profitable while simultaneously reducing the supply of new Bitcoin entering the market.

### Why Was the Halving Mechanism Designed This Way?

Satoshi Nakamoto implemented the halving mechanism to create a deflationary monetary system that mimics the scarcity of precious metals like gold. Traditional fiat currencies can be printed in unlimited quantities by central banks, leading to inflation and debasement of purchasing power over time. Bitcoin's fixed supply and diminishing issuance rate directly contrast with this model, positioning Bitcoin as a potential alternative monetary asset. The halving schedule ensures that approximately 94% of all Bitcoin will be mined by 2024, with the final coins scheduled to be produced around the year 2140.

The halving mechanism also serves as an economic incentive structure that aligns miner behavior with the long-term health of the network. By reducing rewards over time, Bitcoin creates pressure for miners to become more efficient and for the market to establish sustainable price levels. If Bitcoin's value increases proportionally to its decreasing supply, miners can maintain profitability even as rewards shrink. This dynamic has played out across all three previous halving events, with Bitcoin's price generally rising substantially in the months and years following each reduction in block rewards.

## The History of Bitcoin Halving Events

Examining the historical record of Bitcoin halving events provides valuable insights into how these cycles have historically impacted the market. Each halving has had unique characteristics while following a broadly similar pattern of pre-event uncertainty, post-event price appreciation, and evolving market dynamics. Understanding this history helps contextualize expectations for future halving cycles while acknowledging that past performance does not guarantee future results.

### The First Halving: November 2012

The inaugural Bitcoin halving occurred on November 28, 2012, at block 210,000. At this point, Bitcoin was still relatively unknown outside of technology circles and cryptocurrency enthusiasts. The block reward dropped from 50 BTC to 25 BTC, and the price at the time of the halving stood at approximately $12.35, according to historical data from Bitcoin Average. In the months following the halving, Bitcoin's price remained relatively stable before beginning a significant ascent that would ultimately lead to prices above $1,000 by early 2014.

The first halving demonstrated that Bitcoin's fixed supply mechanism could create upward price pressure when demand remained consistent or grew. Early adopters who understood the implications of reduced supply found themselves positioned advantageously as the market began recognizing Bitcoin's scarcity properties. This period also saw the emergence of the first major mining operations, as the reduced rewards made individual mining increasingly unprofitable and pushed the industry toward industrial-scale operations.

### The Second Halving: July 2016

The second halving occurred on July 9, 2016, at block 420,000, reducing the block reward from 25 BTC to 12.5 BTC. By this time, Bitcoin had gained substantially more mainstream attention following the previous bull run. The price at the time of this halving was approximately $650, a significant increase from the first halving's price point. The aftermath of this halving would prove to be historic, as Bitcoin's price surged to nearly $20,000 by December 2017, representing an extraordinary return for early investors.

This second halving period also coincided with increased regulatory attention and the emergence of institutional interest in cryptocurrency. The price explosion drew global media coverage and sparked the first major wave of public awareness regarding Bitcoin. However, the subsequent market correction reminded investors of cryptocurrency's volatility, with prices declining substantially from their peaks. Despite this correction, Bitcoin's price remained far above pre-halving levels, demonstrating the long-term structural impact of reduced supply issuance.

### The Third Halving: May 2020

The most recent halving occurred on May 11, 2020, at block 630,000, cutting the reward from 12.5 BTC to 6.25 BTC. This event took place during an exceptionally challenging global environment, as the COVID-19 pandemic had created unprecedented economic uncertainty. Bitcoin's price at the time of the halving was approximately $8,500, and the market faced significant volatility throughout the preceding months. Despite these challenging conditions, Bitcoin's price subsequently entered one of its most remarkable bull runs in history.

Following the third halving, Bitcoin's price climbed steadily throughout late 2020 and early 2021, eventually reaching an all-time high of nearly $69,000 in November 2021. This represented a gain of over 700% from the halving price and validated the market's ongoing recognition of Bitcoin's scarcity dynamics. The third halving cycle also saw unprecedented institutional adoption, with major corporations adding Bitcoin to their balance sheets and cryptocurrency exchange-traded funds receiving regulatory approval in various jurisdictions.

## How Bitcoin Halving Affects Price

The relationship between Bitcoin halving events and price movements represents one of the most discussed topics in cryptocurrency analysis. While no definitive causal relationship has been proven, historical patterns suggest a strong correlation between halving events and subsequent price appreciation. Understanding the mechanisms behind this relationship helps explain why so many investors pay close attention to the halving schedule and position themselves accordingly.

### The Supply Shock Theory

The most widely cited explanation for halving-related price increases centers on the concept of supply shock. When block rewards are reduced, the amount of new Bitcoin entering the market decreases significantly. If demand remains constant or grows, the reduced supply creates upward pressure on prices. This dynamic mirrors traditional economic principles regarding supply and demand, with the additional factor of Bitcoin's absolute scarcity making the supply reduction particularly significant.

Consider the mathematical implications of halving on daily issuance. Before the third halving, approximately 1,800 BTC entered circulation daily (1800 blocks per day × 12.5 BTC per block). After the halving, this dropped to approximately 900 BTC per day, representing a 50% reduction in daily supply. For a market that had been absorbing this daily supply at existing price levels, the sudden reduction creates an imbalance that can only be resolved through price adjustment or reduced demand. Historically, the market has absorbed this reduced supply at higher prices rather than suppressing demand.

### Historical Price Patterns Following Halvings

Examining the timeline of price movements after each halving reveals patterns that have reinforced the narrative of halving-driven bull markets. Typically, significant price increases have occurred in the 12-18 months following each halving event, with the most dramatic gains appearing in the 6-12 month window immediately following the reduction in block rewards. This lagged effect reflects the time required for market participants to recognize and respond to changing supply dynamics.

The 2017 bull run, which followed the 2016 halving by approximately 18 months, saw Bitcoin rise from the mid-$600s to nearly $20,000, representing roughly a 3,000% increase. The 2021 bull run, following the 2020 halving, saw similar percentage gains from the halving price to the all-time high. These dramatic movements have led many analysts to conclude that halving cycles create predictable windows of opportunity for Bitcoin investment. However, it is crucial to note that these gains have come with corresponding periods of significant correction, with drawdowns of 50% or more occurring within these same market cycles.

### Market Sentiment and Narrative Effects

Beyond the mechanical supply-side factors, halving events influence market sentiment in ways that can amplify price movements. The predictable nature of halving allows investors to plan and position themselves in advance, creating concentrated buying pressure as the event approaches. Media coverage of upcoming halving cycles also tends to increase awareness and attract new participants to the market, further driving demand.

The halving narrative has become deeply embedded in Bitcoin's identity as a monetary asset. Investors increasingly discuss and plan around halving cycles, treating them as fundamental drivers of value appreciation. This collective belief can become self-fulfilling, as the expectation of price increases leads to purchasing behavior that actually drives those increases. While this psychological component is difficult to quantify, it represents an important factor in understanding Bitcoin's halving-related price dynamics.

## Impact on Bitcoin Supply and Scarcity

Bitcoin's monetary policy is unique among global monetary systems, featuring a hard cap of 21 million coins and a predictable issuance schedule that becomes increasingly deflationary over time. Halving events are the mechanism through which this issuance schedule is implemented, and their impact on supply extends far beyond the immediate reduction in daily block rewards. Understanding these supply dynamics is essential for comprehending Bitcoin's potential role as a scarce digital asset.

### Current and Projected Supply Schedule

As of early 2024, approximately 19.5 million Bitcoin has been mined, representing approximately 92.9% of the total supply that will ever exist. The remaining approximately 1.5 million Bitcoin will be produced over the next several decades through the block reward mechanism. However, because halving reduces the reward by 50% with each cycle, the rate at which this remaining supply enters the market becomes progressively slower. The final Bitcoin is not expected to be mined until approximately 2140, representing a timeline that extends well beyond any current human planning horizon.

The supply schedule follows a mathematical progression that creates predictable diminishing returns. After the next halving in 2024, the daily issuance will drop to approximately 450 BTC, meaning it will take longer to produce the remaining Bitcoin than it took to produce the Bitcoin already in circulation. This geometric reduction in supply issuance creates an increasingly rare asset, with the rate of new supply entering the market approaching zero as the 21 million cap is approached.

### Scarcity and Stock-to-Flow Analysis

One framework that has gained significant attention in the cryptocurrency community is the stock-to-flow (S2F) ratio, which measures the abundance of an asset by comparing its existing stock to the annual production flow. Bitcoin's halving directly impacts this ratio by reducing the annual flow while leaving the existing stock unchanged. According to analysis by PlanB and others, Bitcoin's stock-to-flow ratio has historically correlated strongly with market capitalization, suggesting that each halving creates conditions for price increases as the ratio improves.

A higher stock-to-flow ratio indicates greater scarcity, as it takes longer to double the existing supply. Gold currently has the highest stock-to-flow ratio among major assets at approximately 60-70 years, meaning it would take that long to double existing gold supply at current production rates. Bitcoin's halving mechanism is designed to eventually surpass this ratio, with projections suggesting Bitcoin could reach a stock-to-flow ratio exceeding 100 within the next few halving cycles. This mathematical trajectory underpins much of the investment thesis for Bitcoin as a store of value comparable to or exceeding gold.

### Miner Selling Pressure and Supply Dynamics

The halving mechanism affects not only the total supply entering circulation but also the behavior of existing market participants, particularly miners. Mining is an energy-intensive process that requires continuous selling of Bitcoin to cover operational costs, including electricity, equipment, and facility expenses. Before a halving, miners receive more Bitcoin per block, which can allow them to accumulate reserves while meeting their operational expenses.

After a halving, the same operational costs remain, but the Bitcoin revenue is reduced by 50%. This dynamic forces miners to either sell more of their Bitcoin holdings to cover costs or reduce expenses, which often means shutting down less efficient operations. The reduced block reward changes the economic calculus of mining and can lead to increased short-term selling pressure as miners adjust to the new revenue reality. In the medium to long term, higher Bitcoin prices resulting from halving can restore mining profitability, but the transition period often involves significant market dynamics.

## How Halving Affects Bitcoin Mining

The mining sector represents the backbone of Bitcoin's security and transaction validation infrastructure, and halving events have profound implications for this industry. Understanding how halving affects mining provides critical insight into Bitcoin's long-term sustainability and the economic viability of its decentralized network. The relationship between halving, mining profitability, and network security creates important feedback loops that influence Bitcoin's evolution.

### Mining Profitability and Economic Adjustment

Mining profitability depends on the relationship between revenue from block rewards and transaction fees versus the costs of electricity, equipment, and facilities. When halving occurs, revenue per unit of computational power drops by 50%, immediately squeezing profit margins for all miners. This reduction forces efficiency improvements and can eliminate the least efficient mining operations from the market. Historically, hashrate—the total computational power securing the Bitcoin network—has increased despite halving, indicating that mining technology improvements have more than compensated for reduced per-unit rewards.

The economic adjustment to halving is not immediate. Some miners continue operating on accumulated reserves, expecting future price appreciation to compensate for reduced revenue. Others may reduce operations or shut down entirely if their cost structures cannot support the new reward levels. This process typically plays out over months following a halving, with the hashrate often experiencing minor declines before recovering as network difficulty adjusts and market conditions stabilize. The Bitcoin network automatically adjusts its mining difficulty approximately every two weeks to maintain consistent block times, creating a self-regulating mechanism that responds to changes in miner participation.

### Impact on Mining Industry Structure

Halving events accelerate trends toward consolidation and professionalization within the mining industry. Small-scale individual miners increasingly find it difficult to compete with large industrial operations that benefit from economies of scale, access to cheap electricity, and sophisticated operational expertise. The reduced block reward following halving intensifies this competitive pressure, as margin compression favors operations that can achieve the lowest cost per unit of hashrate.

The third halving in 2020 and its aftermath saw unprecedented growth in institutional and publicly traded mining operations. Companies like Riot Blockchain (now Riot Platforms), Marathon Digital Holdings, and Hut 8 Mining raised significant capital and expanded their operations substantially. This professionalization of Bitcoin mining has transformed the industry from a hobbyist-dominated space to a sophisticated, capital-intensive sector. The next halving will likely continue this trend, potentially driving further consolidation as smaller operations struggle with reduced rewards.

### Long-Term Mining Economics and Transaction Fees

As block rewards continue to halve over time, the economic viability of mining will increasingly depend on transaction fees rather than block subsidies. Currently, transaction fees represent a small fraction of miner revenue, typically constituting less than 5% of total earnings. However, as block rewards approach zero, transaction fees must provide sufficient incentive for miners to continue securing the network. This transition represents a critical unknown in Bitcoin's long-term economic model.

Some analysts argue that as Bitcoin's value increases, transaction fees will naturally rise to maintain miner incentives, preserving network security indefinitely. Others express concern that fee-based mining revenue may prove insufficient to support adequate hashrate levels. The resolution of this uncertainty will significantly influence Bitcoin's future trajectory, making the relationship between halving and mining sustainability one of the most important ongoing debates in cryptocurrency economics.

## The Role of Market Cycles and Sentiment

Bitcoin's price movements do not occur in isolation but are embedded within broader market cycles influenced by macroeconomic conditions, investor sentiment, and evolving market structures. Understanding how halving events interact with these larger market dynamics provides a more complete picture of Bitcoin's price behavior and helps contextualize expectations for future cycles.

### Macro Economic Factors and Bitcoin Cycles

Each Bitcoin halving has occurred within a unique macroeconomic context that has influenced its market impact. The first halving in 2012 took place during a period of recovery from the 2008 financial crisis, with loose monetary policy and low interest rates creating favorable conditions for alternative assets. The second halving in 2016 occurred amid growing recognition of cryptocurrency potential, coupled with significant central bank experimentation globally. The third halving in 2020 happened during the COVID-19 pandemic, which spurred unprecedented fiscal and monetary stimulus that many believe accelerated Bitcoin's bull run.

These macro contexts demonstrate that Bitcoin's performance during halving cycles depends not only on supply-side factors but also on broader economic conditions that influence investor appetite for alternative assets. The correlation between Bitcoin and traditional risk assets, particularly technology stocks, has become more pronounced in recent cycles, suggesting that macroeconomic factors play an increasingly important role in Bitcoin valuation.

### Investor Sentiment and Market Psychology

Halving events generate concentrated shifts in investor sentiment that can amplify market movements beyond what fundamental supply-demand dynamics would suggest. The anticipation of halving creates forward-looking optimism that often manifests in price increases in the months preceding the event. This anticipatory buying can create self-reinforcing momentum as rising prices attract additional attention and capital. Conversely, the psychological impact of halving on miner behavior and market expectations can create volatility as participants adjust to new reality.

The emotional dimension of halving cycles deserves particular attention from new investors. The dramatic price movements associated with these events can create fear of missing out (FOMO) during bull phases and panic selling during corrections. Developing a disciplined approach to investment that considers halving cycles without being dominated by short-term sentiment represents a crucial skill for navigating Bitcoin's market dynamics.

### Institutional Participation and Market Maturation

The evolution of institutional participation represents perhaps the most significant change across Bitcoin's halving cycles. The first halving occurred when Bitcoin was primarily a retail phenomenon with limited institutional involvement. By the second halving, some institutional interest had emerged, but major financial players remained largely on the sidelines. The third halving witnessed unprecedented institutional adoption, with major companies holding Bitcoin on their balance sheets, cryptocurrency futures products trading on established exchanges, and regulatory frameworks beginning to accommodate digital assets.

This institutional maturation has several implications for future halving cycles. First, institutional capital brings greater resources and longer time horizons that may smooth volatility while supporting prices. Second, institutional participation introduces correlation with traditional financial markets that can complicate simple halving-based price predictions. Third, the increased regulatory attention that accompanies institutional involvement creates additional factors that influence market behavior around halving events.

This geometric progression suggests that halving effects may become more pronounced over time, as each reduction in supply represents a larger percentage change relative to existing holdings.

The 2140 timeframe when the final Bitcoin is expected to be mined represents a distant horizon, but its proximity in the context of Bitcoin's existence raises important questions about network sustainability and the transition to a fee-only mining model. Understanding that halving cycles are steps toward this ultimate destination helps contextualize Bitcoin's monetary policy as a designed system for creating absolute scarcity within a decentralized framework.

## Common Misconceptions About Bitcoin Halving

As with any significant financial phenomenon, Bitcoin halving has generated numerous misconceptions that can mislead new investors and distort expectations. Addressing these misconceptions directly helps establish accurate understanding and prepares investors to navigate halving cycles with appropriate expectations.

### Misconception 1: Halving Automatically Guarantees Price Increases

Perhaps the most dangerous misconception is that halving events automatically cause price increases. While historical correlation exists, the relationship is not deterministic. Price movements depend on complex interactions between supply dynamics, demand factors, macroeconomic conditions, and market sentiment. Halving reduces supply pressure but does not guarantee corresponding demand increases. Investors who assume automatic appreciation may over-leverage positions and face significant losses if conditions diverge from expectations.

### Misconception 2: All Halving Cycles Produce Similar Returns

Another common misconception involves assuming that all halving cycles produce similar percentage returns. While dramatic bull markets have followed previous halvings, each cycle occurs in a different context with unique market conditions, competitive landscape, and macro environment. The 2024 halving cycle already differs significantly from predecessors due to institutional involvement, regulatory developments, and macroeconomic uncertainty that may constrain returns or amplify them depending on how conditions evolve.

### Misconception 3: Halving Only Affects Miners

Some observers focus narrowly on halving's impact on miners while neglecting broader market implications. While mining economics are directly affected, halving influences investor psychology, market narratives, supply dynamics, and institutional strategy. Understanding halving requires appreciating its systemic impacts across multiple dimensions of the Bitcoin ecosystem rather than isolating its immediate effects on mining profitability.

### Misconception 4: Short-Term Timing is the Most Important Factor

Some investors focus excessively on precise timing around halving events, attempting to buy immediately before and sell immediately after for maximum gains. This approach often fails because markets are unpredictable, and "buy the rumor, sell the news" dynamics frequently produce results opposite to naive expectations. A more robust approach considers halving as one factor among many in long-term investment strategy rather than a singular event requiring precise tactical positioning.

## How to Prepare for Halving Cycles as an Investor

Developing a thoughtful approach to Bitcoin investment that accounts for halving cycles helps position for potential opportunities while managing inherent risks. This preparation involves both practical steps related to investment strategy and psychological preparation for the volatility that halving cycles can produce.

### Practical Investment Strategies

**Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)** represents a particularly appropriate strategy for halving-cycle investing. By investing fixed amounts at regular intervals regardless of price, DCA smooths the impact of volatility and removes the need to predict exact market timing. This approach works particularly well during the months surrounding halving events, when price volatility can be pronounced and emotional decision-making can lead to poor outcomes.

**Long-term positioning** also aligns well with halving-cycle dynamics. Given that significant price appreciation has historically occurred in the 12-18 months following halving, investors with multi-year time horizons may benefit from establishing positions before or shortly after halving events. This approach accepts short-term volatility in exchange for potential structural gains as reduced supply pressure affects market dynamics.

**Portfolio allocation** requires careful consideration given Bitcoin's volatility. While Bitcoin's halving-driven appreciation potential makes it attractive, concentration in any single asset carries significant risk. Most financial advisors suggest limiting cryptocurrency exposure to a small percentage of total portfolio value, with the specific allocation depending on individual risk tolerance, time horizon, and financial situation.

### Understanding Risk Factors

No discussion of halving preparation would be complete without acknowledging associated risks. The historical correlation between halving and price appreciation does not guarantee future results. Market conditions change, regulatory environments shift, and investor preferences evolve. Additionally, the cryptocurrency space remains relatively immature and subject to manipulation, unexpected technical issues, and rapid narrative changes.

Diversification within the cryptocurrency space, careful attention to security practices for storing Bitcoin, and maintenance of emergency reserves independent of cryptocurrency investments all contribute to a more robust investment approach. Understanding that halving cycles, while significant, are one factor among many that influence Bitcoin's long-term trajectory helps maintain appropriate perspective.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Bitcoin Halving

### How often does Bitcoin halving occur?

Bitcoin halving occurs approximately every 210,000 blocks, which translates to roughly every four years. The exact timing depends on network hashrate and how quickly blocks are mined, but the schedule is highly predictable and programmed into Bitcoin's source code. Since Bitcoin's inception, halving has occurred in 2012, 2016, and 2020, with the next expected in 2024.

### What happens when all Bitcoin is mined?

When all 21 million Bitcoin have been mined, which is expected to occur around the year 2140, miners will no longer receive block rewards. At that point, transaction fees will become the sole incentive for miners to continue validating transactions and securing the network. Bitcoin's developers and community continue to debate how this transition will affect network security and what fee structures might be necessary to maintain adequate miner participation.

### Should I buy Bitcoin before or after halving?

There is no definitive answer to this question, as both timing approaches carry advantages and disadvantages. Buying before a halving allows participation in potential price appreciation but risks overpaying if expectations are already priced in. Buying after a halving may offer better entry points if post-halving corrections occur but risks missing earlier gains if prices rise steadily toward the event. A dollar-cost averaging approach that spreads purchases over several months before and after the halving can help mitigate timing risks.

### How does halving affect Bitcoin's inflation rate?

Halving directly reduces Bitcoin's inflation rate by cutting the rate at which new coins enter circulation. When Bitcoin launched, the annual inflation rate exceeded 10%. After each halving, this rate approximately halves. By 2024, Bitcoin's inflation rate will be less than 2%, and it will continue declining with each subsequent halving until reaching zero after the final Bitcoin is mined. This programmatic reduction in inflation contrasts sharply with fiat currencies, which central banks can issue in unlimited quantities.

### Can Bitcoin halving be changed or stopped?

Changing Bitcoin's halving schedule would require a fundamental protocol change accepted by the overwhelming majority of the Bitcoin network. Because Bitcoin operates as a decentralized system without central authority, altering core parameters like the halving mechanism requires broad consensus among miners, developers, node operators, and users. Given Bitcoin's established narrative around its fixed supply and the significant opposition to any inflation-increasing changes, modifying the halving schedule is considered extremely unlikely.

### What impact does halving have on transaction fees?

While halving reduces block rewards, it does not directly affect transaction fees, which are determined by market demand for block space. However, as block rewards decline over time, miners will increasingly rely on transaction fees for revenue, potentially creating upward pressure on fees if insufficient transaction demand exists to provide adequate compensation. Users transacting on the Bitcoin network may eventually face higher fees as the system transitions from reward-based to fee-based security incentives.
